  • Analytical Methods

    Exploratory and preliminary data analysis methods typically involve finding out important aspects of data so that efforts can be focused objectively

    Exploratory and preliminary data analysis is a critical first step in analysing data from a research. The main reasons to do this include: (1) detection of mistakes (2) checking of assumptions (3) preliminary selection of appropriate methods for further analysis (4) determining relationships among the explanatory variables (5) assessing the direction and rough size of relationships between explanatory and outcome variables.

    Data from a research is generally collated into a rectangular array (e.g., spreadsheet or database), most commonly with one row per research subject and one column for each subject identifier, outcome variable, and explanatory variable. Each column contains the numeric values for a particular quantitative variable or the levels for a categorical variable. Data Analysis Methods Exploratory Preliminary have been devised as an aid for looking at data in interesting ways without getting overwhelmed. Therefore, Most of these methods work in part by hiding certain aspects of the data while making other aspects more clear.

    Exploratory data analysis is generally cross-classified in two ways. First, each method is either non-graphical or graphical. And second, each method is either univariate or ultivariate (usually just bivariate). Non-graphical methods generally involve calculation of summary statistics, while graphical methods obviously summarize the data in a diagrammatic or pictorial way. Univariate methods look at one variable (data column) at a time, while multivariate methods look at two or more variables at a time to explore relationships. Usually multivariate analysis will be bivariate (looking at exactly two variables), but occasionally it will involve three or more variables. Beyond the four categories created by the above cross-classification, each of the categories of EDA have further divisions based on the role (outcome or explanatory) and type (categorical or quantitative) of the variable(s) being examined.

    Descriptive and inferential data analysis methods are used after the preliminary exploratory analysis. It involves descriptive and inferential data analysis

    Descriptive and inferential analysis methods help describe a data sample set and draw inferences from the same, respectively. Descriptive Statistics are used to describe the basic features of the data in a study. They provide simple summaries about the sample and the measures. Together with simple graphics analysis, they form the basis of virtually every quantitative analysis of data. With descriptive statistics you are simply describing what is, what the data shows. Inferential Statistics investigate questions, models and hypotheses. In many cases, the conclusions from inferential statistics extend beyond the immediate data alone. For instance, we use inferential statistics to try to infer from the sample data what the population thinks. Or, we use inferential statistics to make judgements of the probability that an observed difference between groups is a dependable one or one that might have happened by chance in this study. Thus, we use inferential statistics to make inferences from our data to more general conditions.

    Descriptive Statistics are used to present quantitative descriptions in a manageable form. Each descriptive statistic reduces lots of data into a simpler summary. Every time a large set of observations is described with a single indicator there is a risk of distorting the original data or losing important detail. Given this limitation, descriptive statistics provide a powerful summary that may enable comparisons across units of analysis. Descriptive statistics provide information about our immediate group of data. Descriptive statistics are applied to populations, and the properties of populations, like the mean or standard deviation, are called parameters as they represent the whole population (i.e., everybody the researcher is interested in). Often, however, a researcher does not have access to the whole population he/she is interested in investigating, but only a limited number (sample) of data instead. Properties of samples, such as the mean or standard deviation, are not called parameters, but statistics. Inferential statistics are techniques that allow us to use these samples to make generalizations about the populations from which the samples were drawn. It is, therefore, important that the sample accurately represents the population. Inferential statistics arise out of the fact that sampling naturally incurs sampling error and thus a sample is not expected to perfectly represent the population. The methods of inferential statistics are (1) the estimation of parameter(s) and (2) testing of statistical hypotheses.

    Interpretation of results is the process of giving specific meaning to data, as data itself does not carry and convey any importance

    Best method for interpreting results of the data analysis involves a hierarchical connection between the research objectives, research process, data and its analysis. This is done in such a way that the person reading is convinced about this results. Broadly a research can be categorized into two groups namely (1) One which has hypothesis (or multiple hypothesis) (2) One which does not have hypothesis (ideally this is rarely a case). Furthermore, researches can popularly be classified as (1) Survey based (2) Experimentation based (3) Observation based. Every type has a different objective and methodology for Data Analysis Methods Interpretation Results. The Interpretation and Results of the report should convey as to how the objective was fulfilled by either confirming to the hypothesis or its rejection. Spanning from sampling and relevance to population under investigation. Sanctity of the data and data collection process. Exploratory analysis, descriptive and inferential analysis. To reinforce repeatability of the research and its adherence to statistical guidelines and rules.

  • Plan Procedure

    Prior to any kind of research activity which requires quantitative data to prove a point, the data analysis plan consists of three things. First, the structure of quantitative data. What would be primary unit of analysis that is “what will be the subject of the study?”. Against This subject of study different variables, factors or data points will be collected during the course of research. Second, the architecture of such variables, their dimensions and their relationship to the subject and relationship between them. Third, how this collection of data will be used to prove or disapprove researcher’s point. That is to say how the data grouped with respect to researcher’s subject will determine whether researcher’s point is proved or not.

    One of the easiest ways of doing this ( Data Analysis Plan Procedure Pre Research ) is to design a spreadsheet popularly known as the master-chart. Usually this master-chart consists of the subject as the first column and its variables as subsequent columns. Last set of the columns consist of different relationships. It is important to note here that from a statistical point of view these relationships define the quantum or size of the sample space required to be studied. Finally, It is also necessary to keep in mind that during the course of research some variables might not be important and some additional variables might be incorporated.
